## What affective forecasting research finds

Gilbert and Wilson's affective forecasting research (late 1990s–2000s) studies how
accurately people predict the emotional impact of future events on their wellbeing.
The consistent finding: people are systematically wrong in a particular direction —
they overestimate how intensely and how long a positive or negative outcome will
affect them. Researchers call this the "impact bias."

Three mechanisms drive the bias:

**1. Focalism** — when imagining a future event, we focus narrowly on that event
and ignore other things that will still be true of our lives when it arrives. A
promotion feels like it will be transformative because we imagine only the promotion;
we don't imagine the meetings, the commute, the unchanged relationships. This is why
the promotion lands more flatly than anticipated.
(Wilson, Wheatley, Meyers, Gilbert, & Axsom, 2000. Journal of Personality and Social
Psychology, 78(5), 821-836. DOI: 10.1037/0022-3514.78.5.821)

**2. Immune neglect** — people have a "psychological immune system" — the mind's
capacity to rationalize, reframe, and find meaning in adverse events — but they
consistently fail to anticipate that this system will kick in. So they overestimate
how bad a bad outcome will feel, because they don't factor in their own resilience
mechanisms.
(Gilbert, Pinel, Wilson, Blumberg, & Wheatley, 1998. Journal of Personality and Social
Psychology, 75(3), 617-638. DOI: 10.1037/0022-3514.75.3.617)

**3. The durability bias** — even when people's direction of prediction is correct
(this bad thing will feel bad), they overestimate how long the emotional impact will
last. Adaptation happens faster than anticipated.
(Wilson & Gilbert, 2003. Affective Forecasting. Advances in Experimental Social
Psychology, 35, 345-411. DOI: 10.1016/S0065-2601(03)01006-2)

## The practical recommendation (calibrational, not soteriological)

Gilbert and Wilson's recommendation is not "detach from outcomes" in a contemplative
sense. It is closer to "calibrate your predictions better" — a corrective for the
specific cognitive bias of impact overestimation. Gilbert's TED talk makes this
vivid: people can synthesize happiness from almost any outcome, yet routinely believe
they can't. The recommendation is largely epistemic (know your biases) rather than
practical-contemplative (train a specific stance toward action and fruit).

## Convergence with the Gītā's vairāgya (for script_gen.py context)

The Gītā's vairāgya is not "predict your outcomes better." It is a trained stance
toward action in which the self-narrative around outcome — the grasping at the fruit —
is recognized as suffering's root and is released at the level of practice and
metaphysical understanding (niṣkāma karma). The convergence with affective
forecasting is structural: both observe that the anticipated emotional payoff of
attainment is systematically not what is delivered. But they diagnose this from
opposite ends: Gilbert/Wilson say "the bias is cognitive, correct it by improving
prediction"; the Gītā says "the attachment is constitutive, release it by training
a different orientation toward the self-as-doer."

This divergence is the interesting edge for Episode 001.
